# How to Learn Anything 3x Faster with Claude: 9 Study Systems

**Core thesis:** Most students use Claude as an answer vending machine. Fast learners configure it as a Socratic tutor that makes them do the thinking — the part that builds memory.

## The Learning Science

Learning speed isn't reading faster. It's:
1. How often you pull a fact from your own head (active recall)
2. How early you catch what you don't understand
3. How well you wire new ideas to old ones

A chatbot that hands you answers kills all three.

## PART 1: Set Up Your Study Brain

### 01. Give Each Subject Its Own Project
**claude.ai → Projects → New Project.** One per course. "Organic Chemistry" is a Project; "Reaction mechanisms" is a chat inside it. Free: 5 projects; Paid: unlimited. Archive after finals. Keep 3-7 active.

### 02. Feed It the Real Material
Upload: syllabus, lecture slides, textbook PDFs, past problem sets, your own notes. **Upload the syllabus first** — tells Claude scope and exam dates. 5-10 focused files beats a 50-file dump.

### 03. Make Claude a Tutor, Not an Answer Key
Set Project instructions once:

```
# Role
You are my Socratic tutor. Your job is to make me learn, not to do my work.

# Hard rules
- Never give the final answer first
- When I'm stuck, give ONE small hint
- Make me explain my reasoning before confirming
- Push back if I ask for the answer — show method, not result

# Style
- One question at a time. Wait for my answer.
- Point out exactly where my logic breaks
```

## PART 2: Learn It Faster

### 04. Prime Before You Study
Before reading: "Give me a one-page map: 5 core concepts, the hardest idea, 4 questions I should answer when done." Now you read looking for answers — active search, not passive reading.

### 05. Run the Feynman Loop
Explain a concept back to Claude like teaching a 12-year-old. Claude stops you the moment you're vague, hand-wavy, or wrong — but doesn't explain it for you. Every stop is a gap you didn't know you had. Loop until no interruptions = mastery.

### 06. Socratic Mode: Make It Ask, Not Tell
"Quiz me. One question at a time. Don't tell me I'm right until I commit. Point out what I got wrong and why. Follow up targeting my weak spot. Start easy, get harder." The discomfort is a **desirable difficulty**.

### 07. Dual-Code with Artifacts
Brain stores pictures + words better than words alone. Name the format: "Build an SVG flowchart," "Build a React simulation I can drag," "Map events and causal connections." Working visuals, not descriptions.

## PART 3: Make It Stick

### 08. Drill Active Recall on Demand
"Generate a 20-question test from my notes: mix recall, application, explanation. Match professor's style. Don't show answers until I finish. Grade me and group mistakes by topic." Missed questions = tomorrow's study list.

### 09. Spaced Repetition + Exam Simulation
- **Flashcards:** "Turn weak topics into 30 flashcards. Export for Anki."
- **Review schedule:** What to re-test on which days, weighted to weak spots.
- **Exam simulator (week before):** "Build a full practice exam in my professor's format. Grade it. Give me a 3-day plan to fix gaps." You walk in having already taken it.

## Slow-Learner Anti-Patterns
1. Asking for the answer instead of the method
2. One endless chat for every subject — no Projects
3. Re-reading notes (recognition ≠ recall)
4. No uploaded material — generic tutor, not your tutor
5. Cramming once instead of spacing
6. Skipping diagrams and simulations
7. Never simulating the exam

## Bottom Line
The fast learners aren't smarter. They configured Claude to make them do the hard part: recall, explanation, catching their own gaps. Every system here is one Project, one instruction, or one prompt away. Start with the tutor instruction or active recall — run it tonight.
