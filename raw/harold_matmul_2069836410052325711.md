# Are you still tuning your LLMs by hand? - An ode to GEPA

**Harold Benoit** (@harold_matmul) — engineering & research @MicrosoftAI

I think GEPA (also called dspy.GEPA) is still highly under-appreciated by the ML community. Credits due to @lateinteraction, @LakshyAAAgrawal (and others). Many thanks to them for being pioneers, and creating the building blocks of "modern AI engineering".

I've used GEPA successfully in the context of pretraining data curation at Microsoft AI, so I thought I'd give a brief overview of why I reach for this tool :)

**One-liner:** dspy.GEPA allows me to tune task-specific LLMs by spending compute instead of human time.

As a deep learning researcher, my productivity and impact is somewhat tied to my ability to keep GPUs busy, and to effectively replace my manual labour by spending compute. Within that mindset, GEPA (and DSPy) are really great tools. But it goes beyond that. Structuring your work around GEPA also acts as a good forcing function to have good experiment hygiene, as it rewards you for creating evals for your tasks (which you should always do!).

## Why and when do we need it?

We, the user, want an LLM to do a task well (e.g. classification, ranking, code generation, grading). The simplest approach is to simply tweak the prompt. Here's why GEPA helps.

### The old way

1. Look at your data, understand the task details (hopefully label and build an eval)
2. Repeat until satisfied:
   - Write the prompt
   - Run inference
   - Analyze failure modes manually (and maybe you have an eval score)

The tuning alone can take a few hours, and is specific to the LLM you picked.

### With GEPA

1. Look at your data, understand the task details (hopefully label and build an eval).
2. Write a dspy.GEPA optimization loop.
3. Run the prompt tuning loop.

You now have a task-optimized prompt. The only manual labour needed is to look at the data. The tuning should take a few minutes, and can be easily rerun for any LLM.

## How does prompt tuning with GEPA work?

At a high level, it is simply a loop optimizing the task prompt, given some grader and a reflection LLM.

The reflection LLM is usually a very strong LLM e.g. Opus or GPT 5.5, capable of understanding and summarizing the mistakes made by the main LLM.

GEPA is the optimizer; its abbreviation means **Genetic-Pareto**, which is a good summary of how the optimizer chooses its best candidates.

The optimization loop is only as good as the objective, and thus we get explicitly rewarded when building good evals!

## A note on evals

The term "eval" is overloaded — it encompasses:

- the train/val samples
- the grader, which is task-specific and can be a composition of human labels and/or a reward model (one or multiple LLM rubric graders usually does the trick)

In the loop, the grader returns both a **score** and **textual feedback**. This is extra flexibility allowed by LLM-driven optimization — useful to give more signal about which solution is preferred.

## Use-cases

- **Quality classification of web pages** — mentioned in the MAI-Thinking-1 paper. For STEM web pages, the grader was an LLM rubric grader judging formatting and reasoning. For code web pages, the grader was mainly human labels.
- **Human+GEPA in the loop** interface to bootstrap a few thousand labels for training an embedding classifier.
- **Reverse-engineering human preferences/priors** that are under-specified. Label ~100 samples (good/bad), tune GPT-5.5 to match human labels, then read the prompt to see if priors can be made explicit.

## Why prompt tuning?

Why not finetune or use an embedding-based classifier? Depends on:

- Quality
- Time to first solution
- Scale (or cost)

The first two are usually most important; prompt tuning with an LLM ticks those easily.

Benefits:

- Tune strong external models without hosting them.
- **Flexibility** — switch main LLM and rerun optimization; compute a Pareto frontier of quality vs cost.
- **Ease of use** — switch between prompts on the fly for different data distributions.
- **No train-inference mismatch** — pick inference settings in advance (e.g. nvfp4, fp8 kv cache, specific kernels) and run GEPA with those numerics.
- **Reasoning** — lowers throughput but needed for hard problems (e.g. math correctness); not possible with embedding classifiers alone.
- **Fast optimization** — minutes to hours depending on search exhaustiveness; solution within a day with quality-cost frontier and ablations before scaling up.

Costs can be reduced by distilling tuned LLM decisions into embedding classifiers or smaller quantized LLMs.
