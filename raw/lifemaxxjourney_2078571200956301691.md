# How To Have Better Conversations

**Author:** lifemaxxers (@lifemaxxjourney)
**Date:** 2026-07-18
**Source:** https://x.com/lifemaxxjourney/status/2078571200956301691

Be the person whose remembered after the party, the meeting, the f*king bar mitzvah - I don't care where it is

THAT incredible specific feeling you get after a genuine connection. You leave it energised rather than drained.
You remember what was said days later. You find yourself thinking about the other person with something close to admiration, even if you only spoke for twenty minutes. That feeling isn't random and it isn't about being naturally charismatic. It's a skill, built from a handful of habits that almost nobody is taught directly, and once you see them you start noticing exactly why most conversations fall flat while a rare few stay with you for years.

## 1. Genuine curiosity is the foundation of everything

Most conversations fail before they even start because one person is waiting for their turn to talk rather than actually listening. You can feel it happening in real time. Someone tells a story and the other person is already halfway through composing their own version, ready to launch into it the second there's a gap. Nothing kills a conversation faster than that dynamic, even if neither person consciously notices it happening.

Genuine curiosity flips that completely. It means actually wanting to know more about what someone just said, not because it's polite, but because you're truly interested in the answer. When someone mentions they just got back from a trip, don't nod and move the conversation onto your own travel stories. Ask what surprised them about it. Ask what almost went wrong. Ask what they'd do differently. People can tell instantly, almost on a subconscious level, whether your questions are performative or real, and real curiosity is one of the rarest gifts you can give someone in conversation. It tells them, without you ever having to say it, that they matter enough to be understood properly rather than just heard.

Curiosity also changes the entire texture of a conversation because it removes pressure from you. You stop needing to have interesting things to say and instead focus on drawing interesting things out of the other person. That's a massive relief if you've ever felt anxious about running out of things to talk about. You never run out of things to ask a genuinely fascinating human being.

## 2. Hold your opinions with humility

There's a particular kind of person who can't get through a conversation without correcting small details, proving a point, or subtly signalling that they know more than the person they're talking to. It's exhausting to be around, even when they're technically right. Nobody enjoys feeling like they're being quietly graded during a conversation.

The better move is holding your own knowledge loosely. Say things like I might be wrong about this, or I've always assumed this but I've never actually checked. That kind of language does something subtle but important. It invites the other person into the conversation as an equal rather than positioning you as the authority they need to agree with. It also means you actually learn things, because people are far more likely to correct you gently or add nuance to a view you've offered humbly than one you've stated as unquestionable fact.

Humility in conversation isn't about being unsure of yourself generally. It's about treating every conversation as an opportunity to be updated rather than an opportunity to perform certainty. The most interesting people I've ever spoken to were the ones most willing to say I don't actually know, tell me more about that. Certainty closes doors. Humility keeps them open.

## 3. Find the common ground before you find the difference

Almost every conversation, even between two people who seem to have nothing in common on the surface, has a thread of shared experience buried somewhere in it if you're willing to look for it. Two people from completely different backgrounds might both understand what it's like to move to a new city and feel like an outsider for the first year. Two people with wildly different jobs might both understand the particular stress of managing other people for the first time. That shared thread, once you find it, turns a polite exchange into an actual connection.

The mistake most people make is leading with difference. They hear an opinion or a background that doesn't match their own and the conversation subtly becomes a negotiation over who's right, or it just goes quiet because there's seemingly nothing to build on. Instead, actively listen for the underlying feeling behind what someone's saying rather than just the specific details. Someone complaining about a difficult boss and someone complaining about a difficult sibling might be describing the exact same emotional experience of feeling unheard by someone with power over them. Find that underlying thread and the conversation suddenly has somewhere real to go.

## 4. Make the other person feel important

This might be the single most underrated skill in all of human interaction. People remember, on some deep and lasting level, how you made them feel far more than they remember what you actually said. If you leave someone feeling genuinely interesting, genuinely valued, genuinely listened to, they will want to be around you again, and they'll speak well of you to other people without you ever having to ask.

This isn't about flattery. Flattery is cheap and most people can smell it immediately. It's about specific, genuine attention. Remembering something they mentioned last time and asking about it. Noticing something impressive about how they handled a situation and actually saying so. Giving them your full attention rather than glancing at your phone or scanning the room while they're mid sentence. Small moments of real attentiveness accumulate into someone feeling like they matter to you, and that feeling is rare enough now that people notice it immediately when it happens.

## 5. Ninety percent of communication isn't words

You can say all the right things and still leave someone feeling cold if your body language contradicts every word coming out of your mouth. Communication is overwhelmingly nonverbal, and most people never think about it consciously, which means most people are unknowingly sending mixed signals constantly.

Eye contact that's present rather than darting around the room tells someone you're actually with them. An open posture, facing towards someone rather than half turned away, signals genuine engagement. Leaning in slightly when they say something interesting communicates more enthusiasm than any verbal response could. Even the pace of your speech matters. Rushing signals nerves or disinterest. A relaxed, unhurried pace signals that you're fully present and in no rush to be anywhere else.

The tone underneath your words often carries more meaning than the words themselves. You can say that sounds difficult in a way that feels deeply caring or in a way that feels dismissive, and the difference has almost nothing to do with the words and everything to do with how they're delivered. Paying attention to your own nonverbal signals, and reading other people's, will change your conversations more than any clever line ever could.

## 6. Novelty

Most people have a rotation of about five conversational topics they cycle through constantly: the weather, work, sports, the news, their kids. It's safe. It's predictable. And it's completely forgettable.

The conversations that stay with you introduce something new. A fresh perspective. A story from a world the other person has never entered. A question they've never been asked. An observation that makes them see their own life differently.

Novelty doesn't mean being weird or contrarian for its own sake. It means bringing a specific detail, a personal anecdote, or a different angle that breaks the script. When you do this well, people's brains light up. They remember the interaction because it didn't feel like every other conversation they've had that week.

Combine these habits and you stop being another face in the crowd. You become the person they think about on the way home. The one they mention to their friends. The one they actually look forward to seeing again.

That's not luck or natural charisma. That's craft — built one conversation at a time.
