How to build your own LLM from scratch in 5 Stages: exact pipeline behind GPT and Claude

I pulled apart how large language models are actually built - the entire pipeline behind ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini - and compressed it into one map.

Follow Substack: movez.substack.com

The lie everyone believes about LLMs: Ask most people how you build a model like Claude and they will say "transformers." It is not. The transformer architecture is largely standardized and freely published. If architecture were the moat, everyone would have GPT-4. In practice it is data, evaluation, and systems that make or break a model - not architectural tweaks.

01. Pretraining - teach model language itself.
Everything starts with one objective: predict the next word (autoregressive language modeling). Do this across enough text and the model absorbs grammar, facts, and reasoning patterns. Tokenization (BPE) comes first. The architecture is a transformer - but that's the part that matters least. Pick the standard tool and move on.

02. Data - where models are actually won.
The pipeline starts with Common Crawl - 250 billion pages, over a million gigabytes. But raw web data is filthy. Processing pipeline: Extract text from HTML → Filter undesirable content → Deduplicate by URL, document, and line → Heuristic filtering → Model-based filtering (Wikipedia-reference prediction) → Data mix with domain reweighting using scaling laws.
Data quality trumps quantity. Collecting data well is the key part of practical LLM work - and the most secretive. Closed datasets dwarf open ones: LLaMA 3 trained on 15 trillion tokens; GPT-4 on an estimated 13 trillion.

03. Scaling laws - spend compute optimally.
You have 10,000 GPUs for a month. What model do you train? Scaling laws answer it predictably. More data and larger models reliably mean better performance. The modern pipeline tunes hyperparameters on small models, then extrapolates to one huge final run. Chinchilla: roughly 20 tokens of training data per parameter is compute-optimal. But accounting for inference cost, the practical ratio rises past 150 tokens per parameter. The bitter lesson: don't over-complicate. Do the simple things and scale them.

04. Post-training - turn a predictor into an assistant.
After pretraining you have something powerful but useless for chat. It completes text but doesn't know it's supposed to answer you. This is where SFT (Supervised Fine-Tuning) and RLHF come in - teaching the model to follow instructions and align with human preferences.

05. Evaluation & Systems - the engineering that ships.
The final stage covers evaluation frameworks, system design for serving, and the infrastructure to run models at scale. This is where models become products.
