# Fable Is the Manager, Sol Is the Worker

**Author:** Nate Herk @nateherk  
**Posted:** 2026-07-10  
**URL:** https://x.com/nateherk/status/2075394620536578128  
**Video:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EthxaDswUFo

GPT-5.6 Sol just dropped, and the benchmarks say it blows Opus and Fable out of the water for a fraction of the price.

Benchmarks are boring though.

So I spent all day driving GPT-5.6 Sol and Claude Fable 5 side by side on real work. Agentic builds in Codex and Claude Code, plus a batch of quick one-off API calls.

Here's everything I found, including the exact costs and where each model wins.

## TL;DR

→ Fable is the manager, Sol is the worker. Fable orchestrating a fleet of Sol agents would be the dream setup.
→ On creative agentic builds, I took Fable's output almost every time. It was just better, and a lot more expensive.
→ On quick one-off API tasks, Sol won 24 to 3, but most of that gap was Fable refusing to answer.
→ When Fable actually answered, it was basically a tie: Sol 0.98, Fable 0.966.
→ Sol is roughly half the cost of Fable and way more token efficient. It feels closer to Opus 4.8 than to Fable Five.

## Three Builds, Same Prompt, Blind Reveal

I gave both models identical /goal prompts with full creative freedom, put Fable on the left in Claude Code and Sol on the right in Codex, and reviewed the outputs blind before revealing which was which.

### 1. The bike game

Prompt: build a genuinely fun, playable, open-world bike game that runs in the browser. WASD to steer, space to bunny hop, Q and E for air tricks, shift for boost.

One version was a top-down bird's eye city. Coins worked, controls worked, you couldn't drive through buildings, but the camera was hard to control and I didn't love the angle. The other was a 3D, almost POV, GTA-style open world with a much bigger map you could actually explore. Clear winner.

The winner was Fable.

→ Fable: 21m 37s, $14.22, ~90,000 output tokens
→ Sol: 23m, $4.50, ~31,000 output tokens

Sol was more token efficient, like it usually is. Here that efficiency didn't win, because Fable's output wasn't a little better, it was significantly better.

### 2. The scroll-stopping website

Prompt: build the most impressive interactive scroll-stopping website you can imagine. Both shipped with sound.

One was a "10 billion years" journey. A 3D interactive background reacting to my mouse, moving through the cloud, the collapse, a galaxy, first light, the swelling, the fuel running out, a supernova boom, ending on the word "you." Immersive, real wow factor. The other, "Vesper Archive," told a similar 3D interactive story but landed a level below.

Fable was the immersive one.

→ Fable: 23m, $19.24, ~80,000 output tokens
→ Sol: ~7m, ~$1, ~20,000 output tokens

Almost 20 times cheaper for Sol. It wasn't 20 times worse though, so if you let Sol spend as much as Fable did, the gap might close. On pure quality, I still took Fable's.

### 3. Five fundamentally different things

Prompt: design five fundamentally different visual elements. Games, presentations, websites, simulations, anything. Each model returned a gallery plus five sister sites.

Fable's five: Singularity (warp space-time, hold to feed, space to detonate), Terra (a flight sim with mouse steering and a day-night toggle), Orbit (a two-rings arcade game with a menu bug that blocked play), Inkflow (a Bored Button style generative art toy), and The Descent (a scroll-underwater story where pressure, sunlight, and temperature change as you go deeper than the Titanic). Solid, but nothing overwhelming.

Sol's five, billed as "impossible objects, five tiny worlds, no frameworks," honestly looked more like a Claude design: Aurora Orchestra (northern lights you can tune), Atlas of Lost Echoes (a map where you recover five echoes), Glyph Heist (a typing game over a grid of contraband words), a genuinely well-designed 5-slide deck, and a tide pool simulation with population and diversity counters. Both galleries had bugs, but Sol had the better spread of ideas.

I gave this one to Sol.

→ Fable: 15m, ~$15, ~65,000 output tokens
→ Sol: 7m, ~$1, ~22,000 output tokens

The ambiguous "make five things" prompt was actually the fastest and cheapest run of the day, which I did not expect.

## The API Round: Sol 24, Fable 3

The builds are how I'll actually drive these models day to day. But I also wanted to hit them straight over the API with quick one-shot, stateless tasks. No agentic loop, no multi-step reasoning. Just "here's a thing, give me an answer," measured on speed and cost.

The record was Sol 24, Fable 3.

Here's what stood out. A lot of Fable's losses weren't losses on capability. Fable flat out refused to answer. It just wouldn't submit. The security guardrails are baked in hard, and they fire on plenty of benign prompts.

Look at the score when each model actually answered:

→ Sol: 0.98
→ Fable: 0.966

Basically the same model on capability. Sol dominated the record because Fable failed to return an answer, not because it was smarter when it did.

And the spend on that batch:

→ Sol: $16
→ Fable: $63

## Why Sol Costs So Much Less

Two things drive the cost gap, and only one is price per token.

Sol is roughly half the cost of Fable per token, about half on input and a little over half on output. Compare Sol to Opus 4.8 instead and they're nearly identical, same input, similar output. That alone tells you where Sol actually sits.

The second thing is token efficiency. Even if Sol and Fable were priced identically, Sol would still come out cheaper because it just uses fewer tokens to get the job done. A lot of that is Codex. Every Codex-versus-Claude-Code comparison I've run has landed the same way, with Codex faster and leaner on tokens.

The cost gap also widens with the harness. Over the API it was about $9 for Sol against $14 for Fable, a $5 gap that shrinks even more in percentage terms. But once you move to full agentic reasoning in Claude Code and Codex, the gap blows wide open.

Speed is more mixed than the cost story. Over the API, Sol's median latency was faster, but its mean was slower. Sol swings, with some runs very quick and some very long. Fable hovered consistently around the 20-second mark. On the agentic builds though, Fable was almost always the slower one.

This is the unit economics point. If a task is a 5, you don't need a model that's a 10, you need a 5 or a 6. For quick stateless API calls, Sol crushes Fable on exactly that math.

Which is also why the honest comparison here is Sol 5.6 versus Opus 4.8, not Sol versus Fable Five. They're not on the same tier. And the naming backs that up. Fable jumped to a 5. Sol, supposedly their most capable model, only got bumped to 5.6, which makes me wonder what they're cooking and saving for GPT-6. That's the release I think actually goes head to head with Fable.

## Manager vs Worker

After the builds, the API round, and a full day of real work, here's where I landed.

Fable is a better manager. I think of it as a co-founder. Sol is a really, really good worker. Fable orchestrating a bunch of Sol agents would be amazing.

What I'd hand to Sol:
→ Price
→ Computer use
→ Playing devil's advocate, because it's more careful about finding bugs and looking at things from different angles
→ Verification, for the same reason, it really thinks about what you asked and does exactly that
→ Speed

What I'd hand to Fable:
→ Creativity
→ Writing, brainstorming, advising, strategy
→ Knowledge work, which I still run on Fable all day
→ Making videos, my two recent "made this entire video" builds went to Fable
→ Not over-engineering, because Sol runs so many tests and will overthink, especially on ultra, where it just kept going and going

Fable also pushes back more. Sometimes sassier, more like a consultant than an order-taker. That's a manager trait, with real pros and cons.

On pure capability, Fable Five is in another tier. Sol is excellent, more comparable to Opus 4.8, clearly better than GPT-5.5, but it's not a Fable Five. If it were, it would be priced like one. It's priced like Opus, and that pricing is telling you something.

So the split I use now: Fable for reasoning and judging, Sol for shipping and executing.

## Where They Actually Rank

Mapping the four models I care about right now:

→ GPT-5.5 and Opus sit close, with GPT-5.5 just behind since I drive most of my work with Opus
→ Sol is clearly ahead of both
→ Then a real gap up to Fable's level

Sol closed the distance to the frontier. It didn't erase it.

## Wrap

Benchmarks are one thing. Getting your hands dirty on the work you actually do every day is another.

This is a feel thing. Some people will say Sol beats Fable hands down, some will say the opposite, and both can be right depending on your use case, your harness, and how you've set up your skills.
