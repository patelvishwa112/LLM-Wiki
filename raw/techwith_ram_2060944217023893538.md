Two Harvard researchers introduce AutoScientists, a decentralized team of AI agents that collaborate, critique & adapt their research strategy over time. 

Instead of following a fixed plan, agents share a common experimental state, propose new ideas, review each other's work before spending compute, form teams around promising directions, and abandon approaches that stop producing results.

Key results:

• Training Optimization: On GPT nanochat training optimization, AutoScientists reaches the same validation performance in just 34 experiments compared to 65 for AutoResearch, delivering a 1.9× improvement in search efficiency. Starting from a stronger baseline where the single-agent system stalls, AutoScientists finds 7 successful improvements in 93 experiments, while AutoResearch finds none across 100 attempts.

• Biomedical Machine Learning: On BioML-Bench, which covers 24 tasks across medical imaging, drug discovery, protein engineering, and single-cell omics, AutoScientists achieves a mean leaderboard percentile of 74.4%. This surpasses the strongest previous biomedical AI research agent by 8.3 percentage points.

• Protein Engineering: For ProteinGym supervised fitness prediction, AutoScientists discovers an extension to Kermut on the ACE2-Spike dataset, improving Spearman correlation from 0.747 to 0.840. The same approach transfers across all 217 ProteinGym assays, raising the benchmark average from 0.657 to 0.700.

The interesting part is not just the performance gains. It is the research process itself. AutoScientists shows how decentralized AI agents can organize around evidence, learn from failures, and continuously reshape their search strategy.
