# Luck runs out on schedule — @0xSpivach

Posted: 2026-07-17
URL: https://x.com/0xspivach/status/2078046857016619053

Every trader who has ever blown up believed, at the peak, that he had figured it out. Not hoped. Believed. The account was up, the trades kept working, and the feeling was indistinguishable from proof.

Here is what almost none of them knew: their luck was not going to run out "eventually," at some vague point in the future. It was going to run out on a schedule. A schedule you can calculate. The same arithmetic that built the streak had already booked the date of the funeral, and the only person not invited was the trader.

This article is about that schedule. Where it comes from, why casinos have been quietly running their entire business on it for a century, why your best month is the most dangerous thing you own, and how to work out which side of the schedule you are actually on.

## The Casino Never Worries About Winners

A casino's edge is small (~1% blackjack well played, ~5% roulette). Thousands walk out winners; the house does not care. It bets on the schedule. With a 2% edge, one hundred bets can go anywhere; ten thousand cannot. Free drinks and comps push you further along that schedule into the region where math wins.

When a player wins for a while, the casino thinks "he has not played enough yet." The market does the same to traders.

## The Arithmetic of the Schedule

With a real edge, expected profit grows linearly with number of trades. Randomness (luck swings) grows with the square root of trades. Over a small sample the square-root term dominates — lucky idiot and genuine sharp look identical. Signal grows faster than noise, so there is a crossover (high hundreds to low thousands of trades for a modest edge) where truth becomes unhideable. That crossover is the schedule.

## Why the Peak Feels Like Proof

Brain reads evidence wrong: confidence compounds faster than data support. Confidence peaks when sample is still too small — exactly when people size up. Blowup arrives where variance returns to normal and position size is at lifetime high.

## Running Out on Schedule

Among a thousand zero-edge traders, after fifty trades chance produces spectacular records. As sample grows, zero-edge accounts get dragged toward zero. Reverse: real edge invisible over fifty trades becomes clear over five hundred and undeniable over five thousand. Noise wins short. Math wins long.

## Finding Your Place on the Schedule

Four questions:
1. How many completed trades, really? Under a few hundred → mostly weather.
2. Does profit survive flat stakes? If not, edge was a few oversized lucky wins.
3. Is size following bankroll or mood? Spikes after wins are a countdown.
4. What is edge per trade? Large edges attract competition; small real edges need volume.

## The Ending Nobody Wants

Given enough trades, everyone converges on what they deserve. Treat best streak like a casino treats its luckiest guest: smile, refuse to believe it, never let it choose bet size. Luck does not run out someday. It runs out on schedule.
