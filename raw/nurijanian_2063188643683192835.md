# The PM as the Context Compressor

By George from prodmgmt.world (@nurijanian)

If you're a PM who spends two days decoding the half-formed thought your exec dropped in the standup, this essay is for you.

You go back to your desk and reconstruct their mental state from context clues, like you're solving a murder. Something came out in passing that you can't tell is a directive, a musing, or a vetoed idea. "I think we should probably think about the pricing model before we commit to this." You can't tell if that's a blocker or a no, so you call your engineering lead, you call another PM, and two days later you have a theory.

## Why this keeps happening

You've probably seen this happen a lot. A leader steps into a conversation about something complicated, starts to engage, feels the pull of how deep it goes, and then drops the unfinished thought anyway. Not because they're careless, but because they have four other deep things waiting and this one wasn't worth finishing.

The failure mode isn't that they don't go deep enough. It's the in-between: they start to go deep, feel the pull, realize the depth is real, and instead of staying silent or finishing the thought, they voice the unfinished version. A half-formed thought is more dangerous than no thought. Silence gives you nothing to misinterpret. A half-formed thought gives you just enough to confidently go in the wrong direction.

The cost isn't paid once. It's paid by every person downstream, every week, for as long as the ambiguity lives. Your engineering lead builds toward one interpretation. The exec looks at it two weeks later and says "that's not what I meant," and a sprint is gone.

This is a structural problem, not a communication problem.

## The compression is supposed to go the other direction

The instinct is to get better access, more face time with the exec and more alignment sessions.

That doesn't solve it. The problem isn't that the exec needs to speak more clearly. The information was never supposed to compress downward in the first place.

Information should compress upward. You synthesize what's happening below, surface the one thing that needs a decision, and frame it at the altitude where the exec operates. They're not supposed to understand the whole system. They're supposed to understand the question you're giving them, in the form you've prepared it.

## That's the job

You are the compression layer, sitting between what the exec asked for and what engineering builds. That's not a soft skill. It's a design problem. And you can build a system around it.

I keep a STAKEHOLDERS.md file with what my execs care about: the metrics they bring up in every review, the concerns they raise unprompted, the way they think about tradeoffs.

Before a meeting, I run Meeting Mastery (/meeting) in PM OS, which generates a single-paragraph brief tailored to that person's context.

The brief covers what's happening, why it matters to them, and what I need from them. I keep it to one paragraph.

That workflow compresses what would have been two days of triangulation into an output I can deliver before the meeting starts. The 2-day deciphering loop becomes a 1-paragraph brief delivered before the meeting.

I also keep a GOALS.md and COMPANY.md that give my workflows enough context to flag when a decision is being framed at the wrong altitude. Before the brief leaves my desk, I run /review (Multi-Perspective Review) in PM OS to pressure-test the framing from multiple angles.

## The cost

Run the math. One day a week reconstructing what the exec meant is ten weeks a year inside the ambiguity, and that's before you count the four to six sprints delivering the wrong thing because the brief was incomplete. That's two months of your year burning on a structural problem you can fix.

You're the compression layer. Build the system or keep paying that cost.
