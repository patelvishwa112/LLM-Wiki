In 2008, most traders looked at mortgage-backed securities and saw a line going up.
They saw a trend. They saw momentum. They ran their time series models. The models said buy. The models were catastrophically wrong.
A small group of traders looked at the same line and asked a different question. Not where is the line going next, but what is actually generating the line? They dug into the underlying mortgage data. They found adjustable rate loans being given to people with no income verification. They found default rates quietly rising in the data before the price chart showed anything. They used that information, which no time series model could have captured on its own, to build positions that made billions when the market collapsed.
This is the most important lesson in all of time series analysis. The line on your chart is a consequence of something happening in the real world. A time series model can tell you what that line has been doing. It cannot tell you what is actually causing it. And the moment the underlying reality changes, your model becomes a liability instead of an asset.
