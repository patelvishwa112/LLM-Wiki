## TL;DR

Top quants on Polymarket model prices as a sequence of states (Markov Chains) to find high-probability trades. The crowd asks "will this resolve YES?" The desk asks "given where price sits right now, what's the full spread of next moves?" 5-step framework: transition matrix → Monte Carlo → calibrate longshot bias → Kelly sizing → limit orders.

## Key Insights

1. **Markov property**: A contract's next price move depends only on its current price, not history. Discretize into 10 states (0-10¢, 10-20¢, ... 90-100¢) and build a transition matrix counting how often price moves from each state to every other.

2. **Monte Carlo simulation**: Start at current state, walk 10K random paths through the matrix. The fraction ending in YES territory is your raw probability. Runs in ~0.1 seconds.

3. **Longshot bias calibration**: Becker's 72.1M trade study: contracts at 5¢ win 4.18% of the time, not 5%. At 1¢, win 0.43% not 1%. Apply empirical calibration or your model overpays for longshots systematically.

4. **Becker's NO rule**: NO outperforms YES at 69 of 99 price levels. If you must trade as taker below 30¢, buy NO — you're riding the bias, not fighting it.

5. **Kelly sizing**: Quarter-Kelly (0.25× multiplier) is the professional standard. Full Kelly is mathematically optimal but psychologically ruinous on drawdowns.

6. **Execution is worth more than your model**: Makers (limit orders) earn +1.12% per trade. Takers (market orders) lose -1.12%. 2.24pp swing. Sports/Crypto/Entertainment markets have 40× wider maker-taker gaps than Finance.

## The 5-Step System

1. **Build transition matrix** — 30-60 days price history → 10 states → count transitions → normalize rows
2. **Run Monte Carlo** — 10K random walks from current state through matrix
3. **Calibrate against longshot bias** — apply Becker's empirical table
4. **Size with quarter-Kelly** — never bet more than math allows
5. **Execute with limit orders** — wait for maker fill, don't cross the spread
