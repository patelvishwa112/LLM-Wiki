The Beginner’s Guide to Physical AI Stack

Physical Ai just had its biggest funding stretch ever. In the first quarter of 2026, startups in robotics and Physical AI raised $16.3 billion across 492 deals, the strongest quarter on record. By late May, companies in the category had already raised more than $23 billion in 2026.
The headlines are everywhere. Figure, Unitree, GR00T, Skild, harmonic reducers, VLAs, teleop, it reads like alphabet soup.
Before we get into the stack, one important clarification: Physical AI is much broader than humanoid robots.
It includes industrial robot arms, warehouse robots, autonomous vehicles, agricultural machines, drones, surgical robots, quadrupeds and other intelligent systems capable of perceiving and acting in the physical world.
And this industry is not starting from zero. Around 542,000 industrial robots were installed in factories in 2024, bringing the operational fleet to roughly 4.7 million machines. Most do not look human, but they already perform useful physical work at scale.
Humanoids are only one branch of this much larger industry. They attract the most attention because they try to combine almost every difficult Physical AI problem inside one machine: perception, reasoning, balance, locomotion, manipulation, touch, power, safety, manufacturing and real-world learning.
Humanoids are not the whole Physical AI market.
They are its most complete stress test.

That is why this guide uses humanoids as the main case study. Once you understand the stack behind a humanoid, you can use almost the same framework to understand a warehouse robot, an autonomous vehicle, a drone or an industrial arm.
So here’s the thing nobody tells you when you start: a humanoid robot is not one industry. It is five different ones stacked on top of each other.
Most companies begin with an advantage in one layer, although the leading players increasingly span several. Once you can see the five layers, the whole field snaps into focus, and you can place almost any company, funding round or product launch on the map.
This is that map. No background needed.
We will go from the robot’s mind, down to its body, down to the parts that make it move, then the data that teaches it, and finally the places where it is actually working today.
Layer 1: The Brain, the AI that decides what to do
What it does
This is the software that turns “pick up the cup” into physical movement.
Not the metal. The intelligence.
When people talk about the “ChatGPT moment for robots,” this is usually the layer they mean.
How it works
The buzzword to know is VLA: Vision-Language-Action model.
In simple terms, it takes what the robot sees, combines it with what you asked, and predicts the action the robot should take.
It is similar to a chatbot, except instead of predicting the next word, it predicts movement.
The model does not always control every motor directly. In many real systems, the high-level model understands the goal and plans the action, while lower-level controllers handle balance, collision avoidance, force and precise joint movement.
The frontier question everyone is chasing is whether one “brain” can work across different tasks and robot bodies, instead of being built for a single machine.
Who to watch
Two broad approaches are emerging. Some companies build the brain and the body together. Others focus on the intelligence layer and try to make it work across hardware made by other companies.
Figure -- its in-house Helix models run Figure’s own humanoids: brain and body together.
Skild AI -- raised $1.4 billion at a valuation above $14 billion for its “omni-bodied” robot brain, designed to work across multiple robot forms.
Physical Intelligence --develops the π family of generalist robot policies and is one of the most widely followed pure-brain efforts.
NVIDIA GR00T --an open research and development platform spanning foundation models, data pipelines, simulation, training and deployment.
Google DeepMind -- develops Gemini Robotics and is partnering with Apptronik on its Apollo humanoid.
Genesis AI -- raised a $105 million seed round and is building a broader robotics platform spanning models, data, simulation and hardware.


Layer 2: The Body, the humanoid makers
What it does
This is the robot you picture: two arms, two legs, a torso and a head.
The companies here are the OEMs, or original equipment manufacturers. They design and assemble the whole machine.
How it works
These are the names that get the demos, magazine covers and largest valuations.
But keep one number in mind: Omdia estimated that global humanoid shipments reached roughly 13,000 units in 2025.
That is fast growth from a very small base. Many of these robots still go to research, education, demonstrations and pilot programmes rather than full production work.
Who to watch
The field roughly splits by geography.
United States
Figure — valued at $39 billion; has worked with BMW and is one of the most closely watched US names.
Tesla — developing Optimus primarily around its own factories; still early and largely internal.
Apptronik — has raised more than $935 million in its Series A and is working with Mercedes-Benz, GXO and Jabil on Apollo deployments and pilots.
Agility Robotics — Digit has moved more than 100,000 totes in a commercial GXO deployment; the company has announced a $2.5 billion SPAC merger.
1X — NEO is a home humanoid offered at $20,000 for early ownership, with remote 1X Experts available to guide unfamiliar tasks.
China
Unitree — the cost leader; the G1 now starts at roughly $13,500 before taxes and shipping, and its Shanghai IPO has received regulatory approval.
AgiBot — ranked by Omdia as the largest humanoid shipper of 2025, with 5,168 units under its methodology.
UBTech — testing and deploying Walker robots across industrial environments, including automotive and electronics manufacturing.
Europe and the rest of the world
NEURA Robotics — developing the 4NE1 humanoid as part of a wider cognitive-robotics platform.
Boston Dynamics — has moved Atlas from a research project toward an industrial product, with 2026 deployments scheduled at Hyundai and Google DeepMind.


Layer 3: The Parts ,actuators, gears, hands and sensors
What it does
This is the layer nobody posts about and everyone depends on.
To move an arm, a robot needs a motor, a transmission to convert that motor’s speed into useful torque, sensors to measure force and position, and a hand or gripper to interact with objects.
A humanoid may have more than 40 actuated joints.
Every one is a small engineering problem.
How it works
Two terms are worth knowing.
An actuator is the robot’s “muscle”: typically a motor, gearbox, sensors, bearings and control electronics combined into a joint module.
A harmonic reducer, also called a strain-wave gear, is a compact precision gearbox commonly used inside robot joints.
It is expensive, difficult to manufacture well and one of the clearest supply-chain bottlenecks in humanoid robotics. But it is not the only one. Motors, roller screws, bearings, tactile sensors, thermal management and long-term reliability are also difficult.
Who to watch
Grouped by what each part does:
Precision gearboxes: Harmonic Drive and Nabtesco in Japan; LeaderDrive in China.
Actuators and joint modules: Sanhua, Tuopu and Inovance, all important parts of China’s growing humanoid supply chain.
Dexterous hands: Sharpa, whose 22-degree-of-freedom Wave hand appears in NVIDIA’s GR00T reference humanoid, along with companies such as Wonik Robotics, Zhaowei and Wuji.
Cameras and LiDAR: Orbbec, RoboSense, Hesai and Sunny Optical.
Force and touch sensing: ATI Industrial Automation, owned by Novanta, and Vishay Precision.
Onboard compute: NVIDIA, particularly Jetson Thor, alongside other edge-compute suppliers such as Qualcomm.
Batteries: CATL, Samsung SDI, LG Energy Solution and Panasonic.


Layer 4: The Fuel,the data that teaches the robot
What it does
A brain is useless until it is trained.
Robots cannot learn from the internet in the same way chatbots do. There is no giant public library of high-quality “robot experience” showing exactly what a machine observed and which motor action followed.
So this layer is about manufacturing training data: teaching robots by showing them what to do.
It has gone from an afterthought to one of the hottest new funding categories in the field.
How it works
There are four main ways to collect the data.
Teleoperation, or teleop
A human uses a rig, headset, controllers or a leader arm to control the robot through a task.
The robot records what it saw, how every joint moved, and which forces were applied.
This produces high-quality data on the real machine, but it is expensive and difficult to scale.
Simulation, or sim
A virtual robot practises thousands or millions of times inside a physics engine.
This is fast, cheap and highly parallelizable. But the virtual world is never exactly the real one. Closing that sim-to-real gap remains a major problem.
Human video and wearables
The robot learns from egocentric video, phones, body sensors, motion capture or sensor-equipped gloves worn by people while they work.
This data is more abundant, but human movement is not the same as robot movement, so translating it into precise robot actions is difficult.
Fleet data
Robots already deployed in the world collect successes, failures, edge cases and human interventions.
Over time, this may become the most valuable data source because it comes from the company’s real robots performing real customer tasks.
Who to watch
XDOF — raised $70 million to build teleoperation data pipelines, collection tools and annotation infrastructure for robotics and frontier AI labs.
Mecka AI — raised $60 million to collect human movement data through body sensors and iPhones.
Genesis AI — combines a robotics model, simulation, data infrastructure and wearable collection hardware.
Physical Intelligence — combines vision-language pretraining with robot-action data and has also explored transferring knowledge from egocentric human video.
NVIDIA Isaac and Omniverse — major platforms for simulation, synthetic data and robot training.
The body makers — Figure, 1X, Tesla, Agility and others are all building in-house data operations because fleet experience may become one of their biggest advantages.


Layer 5: The Proving Grounds, who is actually deploying
What it does
Everything above is potential.
This layer is reality: robots doing useful work for real customers.
How it works
Here is the part that surprises newcomers most.
The robots doing the most real and profitable work today mostly are not humanoids. They are specialised machines built for specific environments and workflows.
Humanoids are moving beyond pure demos, but most programmes are still somewhere between pilot, early deployment and limited production use.
Who to watch
The genuinely scaled, mostly non-humanoid systems:
Amazon — more than one million robots across its operations network; the clearest large-scale robotics ROI example.
Symbotic — approximately $22.3 billion in backlog at the end of December 2025, with Walmart and its warehouse-as-a-service joint venture accounting for the vast majority.
Humanoid deployments, real but still early:
Figure at BMW — more than 1,250 hours of runtime and work connected to the production of over 30,000 vehicles, but not yet a large multi-site humanoid fleet.
Apptronik at Mercedes-Benz, GXO and Jabil — commercial relationships and pilot deployments.
Agility Robotics at GXO, Toyota, Schaeffler and Mercado Libre — one of the clearest examples of humanoids moving into commercial operations.
UBTech — industrial customer programmes in China alongside early international manufacturing trials.
What newcomers get wrong
“Humanoids are working in factories now” is true, but incomplete.
A small number are performing real tasks in real facilities. Some have moved into commercial agreements.
But a pilot is not the same as a scaled fleet.
The proven, boring, money-making robots are still the wheeled and armed machines already operating throughout warehouses and factories.
That gap between the hype around humanoids and the reality of purpose-built automation is one of the most useful things to understand about where the industry stands today.
The whole map, in one breath
The next time you read a robotics headline, place it on the stack:
Brain — the AI that decides: Figure Helix, Skild, GR00T, Physical Intelligence.
Body — the humanoid makers: Figure, Tesla, Unitree, Apptronik, Agility, 1X.
Parts — actuators, gearboxes, hands and sensors: Harmonic Drive, LeaderDrive, Sharpa.
Fuel — the training data: teleoperation, simulation, human video, wearables and fleet experience.
Proving grounds — who is deploying: Amazon, Symbotic and the growing group of humanoid deployments.
The money is flooding into all five layers at once, which is why the field feels chaotic.
But the field itself is not chaotic.
It is a stack.
The brain decides. The body carries it. The parts move it. The data teaches it. Deployment proves whether it works reliably and economically.
That is the map.
Everything else is detail.
