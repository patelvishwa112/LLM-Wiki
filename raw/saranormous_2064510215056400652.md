# The Untrainable

**Author:** @saranormous (sarah guo, Conviction VC)
**Source:** https://x.com/saranormous/status/2064510215056400652
**Also:** https://open.substack.com/pub/saranormous/p/the-untrainable
**Date:** 2026-06-10

---

The mid-2026 investor's version of AI psychosis is a despair that nothing is investable, that we should put all our money into Anthropic and Nvidia and go home. I have never felt it.

## The Despair Isn't Stupid

The logic runs: if the model keeps getting better at everything, then every company built on top of one is a thin wrapper waiting to be absorbed, and the only value that survives is the compute and the frontier weights.

## Software Engineering as Exhibit A

Devin shipped in 2024 solving thirteen percent of the tasks on the standard software benchmark, and was largely dismissed. A year and a half later the best agents hit the high eighties. MIT study across 100,000+ developers: coding agents lifted how much code got written by roughly 180%, and how much actually shipped by about 30%. Writing got cheap. The rest still runs through a person, and it matters.

A benchmark is a thing you can measure, and a thing you can measure is a thing you can train against. Coding agents matured first: a compiler is a free verifier, a test suite is a free verifier. But passing the test never told you the change was the right one for a decade-old codebase with three undocumented reasons that module exists.

## Slow Moats

That kind of correctness can't be read off a leaderboard. You find out whether a system that complex works by running it in the world long enough to learn, and a smarter model doesn't make the world run faster. Noam Brown: the only sure way to evaluate an agent over a one-year horizon may be to run it for a year.

Real automation isn't only the model getting better. It's the product, the model, the workflow, and the firm moving together, and three of those four move at the speed of an organization. Every company has every engineer on frontier coding models, and not one has changed its eng org at anything close to that speed. Adoption took a quarter. The rebuild is taking years.

## The 2x2

What's legible is what's leaving. Anything measurable is already on its way to commodity. A token spent answering a generic question is worth almost nothing, while a token spent reasoning over your company's data is worth much more.

- Saturated + public answers → commodity tokens, open models win
- Frontier + public answers → labs win (coding benchmarks live here)
- **Frontier + private correctness → the prize corner** — custom models dominate

## Two Walls

The lock is the environment: you only get to verify whether AI did something useful inside a system once you're trusted inside it, after the security review, the integration, the contract with your name on the outcome. The deadbolt is the user. A majority of American doctors now open OpenEvidence every day, and no amount of compute buys that. A lab can train a flawless medical model tomorrow and still have no way into the physician's habit.

## The Work That Earns a Place

An application earns its place in the untrainable corner by doing unglamorous work: arranging a company's private reality so a model can act on it, handing the model the tools to act, working with the customer to change the reality of its workforce.

White-shoe law firm example: M&A practice runs ~1,000 deals/year. Each practice area has its own deal shape. The real problem is running every practice area in parallel. Transforming a firm like that isn't a single task you can write an eval for.

## Pricing Outcomes

Sierra charges when its agent resolves a customer's issue and nothing when it kicks to a human. Cognition's Devin has a "performance guarantee." You can only offer that for outcomes in a system you're trusted inside.

## Serving Isn't Commodity Either

The best AI-native companies concentrate serving on one or two providers (Baseten or Fireworks) because cost per token commoditizes while reliability under real traffic and guaranteed access to scarce compute do not.

## The Lab-as-Supplier Fear

Only works if the model layer is a single-player game. It's a three-and-a-half-way death match. Consumer chat proves it: ChatGPT held its lead through years of competition, losing share now to Gemini via Android/Search, not a better model. Anthropic, rated as having the best model, is barely a factor in consumer chat.

## Authority to Define "Good"

The senior lawyer writes the legal benchmark. Defining a safe clinical answer falls to a physician. Resolved means whatever the company that already owns the customer says. Harvey publishes one for law, Sierra for voice agents, OpenEvidence for clinical answers. A foundation lab can't author this however smart it gets — that standing only exists inside the field.

## Keep Stepping

The absorption frontier keeps rising. On a narrow task with private data and your own evals, you can train to the frontier and beat general models where it counts. Competing on the general model is a capital war you lose. Offense is even harder: choosing what to build. The model can't tell you what's worth pointing it at. Maybe intent is an even scarcer input than compute.

## Conclusion

The despair is half right. The thin wrapper layer really is being absorbed. It is wrong about what that leaves. Intelligence keeps getting cheaper, and value keeps sliding toward the few places a model can't reach. The untrainable is value with history. Get inside one, do the unglamorous translation, and start writing down what good means there, because someone is going to.
