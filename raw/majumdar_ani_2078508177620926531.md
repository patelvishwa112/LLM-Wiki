# Understanding Video Models: Part III - RL Post-Training

**Author:** Anirudha Majumdar (@Majumdar_Ani)
**Date:** 2026-07-18
**Source:** https://x.com/majumdar_ani/status/2078508177620926531
**Article:** https://x.com/Majumdar_Ani/article/2078508177620926531
**Engagement (at capture):** ~55 likes, 6 reposts, 4.4K views

In this post, I will discuss post-training methods for video models. Similar to LLMs, there is an emerging set of methods that seek to improve various aspects of the pre-trained model: adherence to the prompt, motion quality, visual consistency, inference speed, etc. The primary techniques in this realm parallel the ones used for LLMs: reinforcement learning with (1) human feedback and (2) verifiable rewards.

At a high level, RL for video models uses some version of the following objective:

sup pθ   E[c~Dc, v~pθ(·|c)] [ r(v, c) − β KL(pθ(·|c) || p_ref(·|c)) ]   [Eq. (1)]

where v is a video generated using context c, and p_ref is a reference (e.g., pre-trained) video model. The early papers on RL for diffusion models (c. 2023 onwards) were focused on learning from human feedback. More recently, there has been an emerging line of work on using verifiable rewards for RL, particularly for improving geometric consistency.

## RL with human feedback (RLHF)

**Diffusion-DPO and Flow-DPO.** Early RLHF methods for diffusion models adapt direct preference optimization (DPO; Rafailov 23) to diffusion models. DPO exploits the well-known observation that Eq. (1) can be solved in closed form to write the reward in terms of the policy (generative model), and thus bypasses the need to explicitly train a reward function from human preference data. The key challenge in adapting DPO to diffusion models lies in compute likelihoods from diffusion models. Diffusion-DPO (Wallace 23) uses an evidence lower bound to derive a differentiable approximation.

The paper "Improving Video Generation With Human Feedback" (Liu 25) uses the connection between denoising diffusion models and flow matching to propose Flow-DPO. They also develop a reward-weighted regression method (Flow-RWR) and an inference-time guidance method (Flow-NRG), but find that Flow-DPO exhibits superior performance.

The key contribution of the Flow-DPO paper is data-centric. They create a large-scale human-labeled preference dataset consisting of 182K examples spanning multiple dimensions of preferences and 12 video generation models. Flow-DPO used this dataset for DPO, leading to very strong improvements in performance; see above for a qualitative example of video generation before and after RL. The SkyReels-v2 video model also uses Flow-DPO for improving motion quality.

**GRPO for flow matching.** On-policy RL methods have also recently been applied to flow matching models for image and video generation. Flow-GRPO (Liu 25) uses group relative policy optimization (GRPO; Shao 24). The key challenge with adapting GRPO to flow matching is the deterministic nature of the "action" (i.e., velocity) that flow matching follows. This limits exploration — which is critical for RL — while also preventing the calculation of the KL divergence regularization in Eq. (1). In order to get around these challenges, Flow-GRPO translates the ordinary differential equation (ODE) that flow matching uses to sample from into a stochastic differential equation, while matching the marginal distribution of states visited by the ODE. Flow-GRPO also reduces the number of denoising steps during training (while keeping the number of steps for inference high) to increase computational efficiency.

Dance-GRPO (Xue 25) was released concurrently with Flow-GRPO and has many of the same ideas. They demonstrate the benefits of GRPO for improving the quality of video models; see below for an example.

**Reward Models for RL.** Strong reward models are critical for RL. In addition to CLIP-based reward functions, video model RL is performed using VLMs fine-tuned on preference data (e.g., using a Bradley-Terry loss). As an example, RewardDance (Wu 25) fine-tunes a VLM to take an image or video pair and gauge whether one of the inputs is better than the other along a particular axis (e.g., visual consistency or text-to-image alignment). The score is simply the VLM's token probability of 'Yes'. They find that including reasoning traces in the training data improves performance. VideoReward (Liu 25) is another VLM-based reward model that produces judgements across multiple axes of quality.

## RL with verifiable rewards (RLVR)

Compared to LLMs, the literature on RLVR for video models is still very sparse. Even the notion of "verifiability" is not entirely clear. Here, I will discuss methods that post-train video models using rewards that are verifiable in the sense of being objective (i.e., not based on human preferences), e.g., geometric consistency across video frames or smoothness of camera motions.

**Epipolar-DPO (Kupyn 25)** uses a simple epipolar geometric check on pairs of video frames: given a point x in one frame and the corresponding point x' in another frame, do they satisfy the epipolar constraint (x')^T F x = 0, where F is the fundamental matrix relating the two frames? Deviation from this constraint can be captured by the Sampson epipolar error (Sampson 82) once the fundamental matrix has been estimated. Epipolar-DPO curates an offline dataset of video pairs that are labeled with preferences computed using the epipolar error. This dataset is then used for post-training the base model using the Diffusion-DPO or Flow-DPO approaches highlighted above.

**VideoGPA.** Epipolar-DPO uses pairs of frames to estimate geometric consistency and only tries to impose the epipolar constraint. In contrast, VideoGPA (Du 26) attempts to impose scene-level geometric consistency. It uses a geometric foundation model to extract camera poses and point clouds which are aggregated across frames; the aggregated point cloud is then reprojected back to every frame in order to measure consistency with the original frames. Similar to Epipolar-DPO, VideoGPA constructs a preference dataset that is used for DPO.

**VGGRPO (An 26)** addresses two challenges with VideoGPA: (1) the assumption that scenes are static and (2) the need to decode video latents into RGB images in order to compute the reward. For the first challenge, they utilize geometric foundation models that work with dynamic scenes. Similar to VideoGPA, they compute a scene point cloud aggregated from all the video frames and then reproject this back into the original frames; however, only the static portions of scenes are aggregated into the point cloud. The second innovation of VGGRPO is to train a lightweight adapter that maps video latents to latent features of a given geometric foundation model. This means that RL training (using GRPO) can be executed directly in the video latent space. The videos above qualitatively show improvements in consistency from VGGRPO.

## Concluding thoughts

So far, RL methods for video models are largely adaptations of methods used for LLMs: DPO, PPO, GRPO, etc. It will be interesting to see if this state of affairs continues, or if entirely new methods will be developed. RLVR in particular is a very nascent area, and I expect that there will be a lot of activity here. For LLMs, post-training is critical to eliminating the undesirable behaviors that pre-trained models demonstrate. While RL post-training currently plays a much more limited role for video models, I suspect that this will change in the coming years.
