# Mechanistic Interpretability Is Not the Whole Interpretability Field

By @giangnguyen2412 | June 21, 2026

URL: https://x.com/giangnguyen2412/status/2068743875527844200

---

**Core Message**

The author (who works on interpretability at Guide Labs AI) argues that many people now enter the field through **mechanistic interpretability** (mech interp) — especially via sparse autoencoders (SAEs) and circuits — and mistakenly treat it as synonymous with the entire field of interpretability/explainability. 

This conflation is risky because mech interp is only one recent branch of a much older, broader field. Ignoring the history leads to rediscovering old failures, using the wrong evaluation methods, and unnecessarily limiting the available tools.

**What Mechanistic Interpretability Actually Is**

Mech interp focuses on **reverse-engineering** a model’s internal computation into a human-readable algorithm. It examines weights, activations, and connections to recover the “circuit” implementing a behavior (e.g., the circuits thread by Olah et al. 2020, superposition by Elhage et al. 2022, and SAEs by Bricken et al. 2023).

The term is often used loosely — sometimes as a specific causal method, sometimes as a cultural “vibe.” The cultural usage is what blurs important distinctions.

**Pre-Mech Interp History (Interpretability Predates Circuits)**

The thread outlines several earlier approaches that are **not** mechanistic:

- **Feature Attribution**: Assigning credit for a prediction to input features. Examples include saliency maps (Simonyan 2013), Layer-wise Relevance Propagation, DeepLIFT, **Integrated Gradients** (Sundararajan 2017 — where the author’s own work sits), LIME, SHAP, and Grad-CAM. These explain input-to-output behavior without claiming to recover internal algorithms.

- **The Faithfulness Crisis**: Many attribution methods failed “sanity checks” (Adebayo et al. 2018 showed they often produced similar heatmaps even on randomized models). Related issues appeared in attention explanations and input perturbations. The field spent years learning that plausible-looking explanations can still be useless.

- **Inherently Interpretable Models**: Instead of post-hoc explanations, design the model to be interpretable. Examples: Prototype Networks (ProtoPNet, Chen et al. 2019) that classify by matching parts to learned prototypes, and Concept Bottleneck Models (Koh et al. 2020) that predict human concepts first, then use them for the final prediction. Cynthia Rudin has been a major advocate for this approach in high-stakes settings.

- **Probing**: Training simple classifiers on internal representations to test what information they encode (Alain & Bengio 2016, Hewitt & Manning 2019). Closer in spirit to mech interp but predates the term.

- **Counterfactual Explanations**: Finding the smallest change to an input that would change the output (Wachter et al. 2017, DiCE, actionable recourse). These are contrastive and don’t open the black box.

- **Conceptual Foundations**: Papers like Lipton (2016) on the “mythos” of interpretability, Doshi-Velez & Kim (2017) on rigorous evaluation, and Rudin (2019).

Mechanistic interpretability (starting prominently with Olah’s 2020 “Zoom In: An Introduction to Circuits”) is the newest major branch.

**Why the Conflation Is Dangerous**

1. **Rediscovering known failures** — e.g., building attribution-like methods without reading the sanity-check literature (similar issues have already been shown for some SAE work).
2. **Wrong (or missing) validation** — Mech interp often uses causal interventions (patching, ablations). Attribution has its own axioms (completeness, sensitivity, implementation-invariance). Assuming everything is mech interp leads to mismatched or absent validation.
3. **Shrinking the toolbox** — Many questions are better answered by attribution, probing, concept-based methods (TCAV, concept bottlenecks), example-based methods (influence functions), or counterfactuals than by circuits alone.

**Rough Taxonomy of Approaches**

- **Attribution**: Integrated gradients, SHAP, LIME
- **Probing**: Linear/structural probes
- **Concept-based**: TCAV, concept bottleneck models
- **Example-based**: Influence functions, training-data attribution, prototype networks
- **Counterfactual**: “What would change the outcome?”
- **Mechanistic**: Circuits, superposition, SAEs

Methods sometimes overlap (e.g., attribution patching in circuit discovery), but the distinctions matter.

**Takeaway**

If you entered interpretability through mech interp, read the older literature. Recommended starting points:
- Saphra & Wiegreffe (2024) — “Mechanistic?”
- Adebayo et al. (2018) — sanity checks
- Lipton (2016) — what interpretability even means

The field is larger than one method. Good work chooses the tool that fits the question.
