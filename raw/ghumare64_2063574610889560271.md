The job description quietly rewrote itself.

In 2023, an "AI engineer" implemented an API key to a prompt template and shipped. The hard part was product, not the model. By 2026 the model is the product surface, and the people who get paid the most can move up and down the stack without flinching from the matrix multiply at the bottom to the autoscaling serving fleet at the top, and back down the moment something breaks at 2 a.m.

Here's the tell. Almost everyone in infra can recite that prefill is compute-bound and decode is memory-bound. Far fewer can trace that sentence to the operations it describes which matmuls dominate prefill, why decode spends its life waiting on HBM bandwidth, what sits in the KV cache, and why that cache grows linearly with sequence length and batch until it eats the GPU you were trying to save. That gap, between knowing the slogan and knowing the mechanism, is the whole game.

Most courses don't close it because they're built backwards: watch a video, run a notebook, import a framework that does the interesting part for you. You finish with a vibe, not a mechanism, and nothing you can reuse on Monday.

ai-engineering-from-scratch is built the other way around, and that's the part worth explaining.

## The method: build it, then ship the tool

Every one of the 503 lessons follows the same loop:

**BUILD IT:** you write the algorithm from raw math first. Backprop. The tokenizer. Attention. The agent loop. No framework, no pip install doing the part you came to learn. Then, and only then, you run the same thing through the production library and see what the library was hiding.

**SHIP IT:** every lesson ends in a reusable artifact: a prompt, a skill, an agent, or an MCP server, sitting in the lesson's outputs/ folder. Not homework you throw away. A real tool you keep and drop into your editor.

That second half is the part people miss. You don't just understand loss functions after the deep-learning chapter, you walk away with a prompt-loss-function-selector and a prompt-loss-debugger you can paste into any model the next time a training run goes sideways. Finish the tokenizer lesson and you keep a skill-tokenizer. Finish KV-cache, you keep a skill-inference-optimizer. Roughly 500 of these ship across the curriculum, and they're agent-native: they read as SKILL.md, so they drop straight into Claude, Cursor, Codex, OpenClaw, Hermes, or anything that speaks the format.

So read what follows as two things at once: a roadmap of what to understand in 2026, and a list of the tools you keep for understanding it. Don't go top to bottom like a textbook, find the layer where your mental model goes fuzzy, usually the one just under where you work, and build that one by hand.

## Layer 1: The math you actually use

Not a degree. The four things that show up in every stack trace: linear algebra, gradients, probability, and automatic differentiation. Build a tiny autodiff engine yourself, a handful of classes that record operations and replay them backward and backpropagation stops being a black box you trust and becomes a mechanism you can put a print statement inside.

Build: Chain rule & automatic differentiation

## Layer 2: Deep learning core

Write backprop through a small MLP by hand, chain rule expanded for every layer, and you inoculate yourself against a whole category of bugs. Vanishing gradients, dead ReLUs, why init matters, why residual connections exist, these stop being trivia and become things you see in the numbers. "The loss is NaN" turns from a panic into a diagnosis: log of zero, divide by tiny, or a learning rate that launched a weight to infinity, and you know which by where it shows up.

Keep: prompt-loss-function-selector, prompt-loss-debugger. Build: Backpropagation from scratch

## Layer 3: The transformer, and the cache you pay for

Attention is less exotic than it sounds: a matmul to score every query against every key, a softmax, another matmul to mix the values. Build it and the quadratic cost is obvious, the score matrix is sequence-length squared, which is why prefill saturates the GPU's FLOPs.

Then build the KV cache and the other half of the bill appears. At decode you generate one token at a time; without the cache you'd recompute keys and values for the whole history every step. Its size is roughly: 2 × batch × n_kv_heads × sequence_length × d_head × dtype_bytes. The 2 is K and V. With grouped-query attention n_kv_heads is much smaller than the head count — that's the entire reason GQA exists, to shrink this number. Plug in a real model and you see why a long context at high batch is what OOMs you, not the weights, and why decode is bandwidth-bound: every token step streams the whole model plus the whole cache out of HBM, so you wait on bytes, not flops. FlashAttention then earns its keep by tiling the computation so the score matrix never fully lands in HBM.

Keep: skill-inference-optimizer. Build: Self-attention from scratch → KV cache + FlashAttention

## Layer 4: An LLM, end to end, from scratch

The centerpiece. About 500 lines of PyTorch: a byte-pair-encoding tokenizer, a decoder-only GPT (same architecture family as GPT-2/3/4, smaller), the pretraining loop that moves the loss, then SFT and DPO on top. Once you've watched the loss curve respond to learning rate, seen what vocabulary size does to sequence length and cost, and written the DPO gradient step yourself, every "fine-tuning" pitch in the market reads differently.

Keep: skill-tokenizer, prompt-tokenizer-analyzer. Build: Tokenizers → Pretrain a mini-GPT → DPO from scratch

## Layer 5: LLM engineering

The application layer, where most money is made and lost. Context engineering is a real constraint: the window is a fixed token budget, and how you spend it: system prompt, retrieved chunks, history, few-shot, the query sets both quality and cost on every call. RAG is trivial to demo and genuinely hard to make precise; recall, reranking, and faithfulness are where naive RAG dies, and that's the lesson.

Keep: skill-advanced-rag, prompt-advanced-rag-debugger. Build: Context engineering → Advanced RAG

## Layer 6: Multimodal

Vision-language is table stakes; the engineering is the bridge. An image is cut into patches, each patch becomes an embedding, and those embeddings have to become tokens the LLM can attend to alongside text. CLIP learns a shared space with a contrastive objective; Q-Former and gated cross-attention are different answers to fusing modalities without retraining everything.

Build: CLIP and contrastive pretraining

## Layer 7: Tools and protocols (the 2026 inflection point)

The genuinely new layer this year. 2025 standardized the plumbing: MCP gave models a uniform way to discover and call tools; A2A gave agents a uniform way to talk to each other. Both moved from "interesting spec" to "thing your security team asks about."

The detail that matters if you ship: the November 2025 MCP authorization spec made Client ID Metadata Documents the recommended enrollment path and demoted dynamic client registration to a backwards-compatible fallback. Tokens are audience-pinned per server; JWKS handling is a refresh-not-rotate problem on the resource server; audience validation is the defense against a token minted for one server being replayed against another. This is the layer that gets your agent through an enterprise review instead of stuck in it, and you finish it holding a working auth skill, not a diagram.

Keep: skill-mcp-auth (the production auth surface as a drop-in skill). Build: MCP fundamentals → MCP auth in production → A2A protocol

## Layer 8: Agent & Harness engineering

The agent loop is the new control flow: plan, act, observe, verify, repeat. Write one by hand about 120 lines of pure Python and you learn the hard parts aren't the LLM calls. They're stopping the loop, remembering across a context window too small for the task (virtual context, the Agentmemory idea), and verifying a step worked before you build on it. Verification gates and an observation budget are what separate a flashy demo from something that survives a long task without drifting.

Keep: skill-agent-loop. Build: The agent loop → Memory and virtual context (MemGPT)

## Layer 9: Autonomous systems

The frontier: long-horizon agents, self-improvement loops, and the guardrails they demand the moment they work. The number to watch is task length, METR frames capability as how long an agent stays coherent before it falls apart, and that horizon keeps climbing. Everything interesting and everything worrying lives in extending it safely.

Build: Long-horizon agents

## Layer 10: Infrastructure and production

Where the infra crowd lives, and where every earlier choice shows up as latency, goodput, or a GPU bill. Speculative decoding is the clearest case of theory paying rent: a small draft model proposes several tokens, the big target verifies all of them in one forward pass, and you commit the longest accepted prefix. Because decode is bandwidth-bound and that verification pass costs about what one normal token does, the speedup scales with how often the draft is right (EAGLE-3 pushes the acceptance rate hard). Prefill/decode disaggregation goes further and runs the two phases on separate hardware, because compute-bound prefill and bandwidth-bound decode want different machines, mixing them on one pool wastes both.

Keep: skill-vllm-scheduler-reader. Build: vLLM serving internals → Speculative decoding (EAGLE-3) → Prefill/decode disaggregation

## Layer 11: Safety and alignment

An engineering discipline, not a checkbox at the end. Reward hacking and Goodhart explain why your eval starts lying the moment you optimize against it. Sycophancy is a measurable RLHF side effect, not a personality quirk. Deceptive alignment and sleeper-agent behavior have real experiments behind them. Constitutional AI / RLAIF is a practical mitigation. You can't defend against failure modes you can't name.

Build: Constitutional AI / RLAIF

## Layer 12: Capstones

Then you build the real thing, end to end, and the artifact stops being a single skill and becomes a system: a terminal-native coding agent, a production RAG chatbot for a regulated vertical, a speculative-decoding inference server, an MCP server with a registry and governance. Portfolio-grade, every earlier layer forced to connect.

## The bet underneath all of this

Frameworks churn. The serving stack you learn this quarter has a new favorite next. What doesn't churn is the layer underneath: attention is still a matmul and a softmax, the KV cache still grows the same way, gradients still flow backward the way they did in 1986. Learn the mechanics once and every new framework becomes a thin shell over things you already understand.

That's the case for building from scratch, not nostalgia, leverage. And because every lesson ends in a tool you keep, you don't just leave with understanding. You leave with ~500 working prompts, skills, and agents wired into the editor you already use.

Build it. Pass the test. Keep the artifact.
